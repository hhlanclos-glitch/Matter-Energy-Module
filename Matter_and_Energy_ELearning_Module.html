Lesson 1 · TEKS 8.6A

Conservation of Mass

It's tempting to think that when a log burns down to a small pile of ash, matter just disappears — the ash weighs a lot less than the log did. But the "missing" mass didn't vanish. It left as invisible gases that escaped into the air. If you could trap every gas particle and weigh it along with the ash, the total mass would be exactly the same as the log you started with.

That's the law of conservation of mass: in a closed system — one where nothing can enter or leave — the total mass of the substances present before a change equals the total mass present after the change. Atoms aren't created or destroyed during a physical or chemical change. They're just rearranged.

C = Carbon H = Hydrogen O = Oxygen Same six atoms, same 50 g, before and after — just rearranged.

Lesson 2 · TEKS 8.6D

Spotting a Chemical Change

An ice cube melting on the dock and a steel piling rusting in the bay water both look like "change." But only one of them produces a brand-new substance.

When ice melts, it's still H₂O — just in a different state. That's a physical change: the size, shape, or state of matter changes, but no new substance forms, and it's often reversible. When iron rusts, the iron atoms bond with oxygen to form a completely new compound, iron oxide. That's a chemical change, and it usually can't be undone.

Scientists look for these clues as evidence that a chemical change has happened:

🫧 Gas production
Bubbles form, even without heating
🌡️ Temperature change
Gets hot or cold with no outside heat source
🎨 Color change
The substance turns a new color
🧪 Precipitate forms
A solid appears in a clear liquid
👃 New odor
A new smell appears

Lesson 3 · TEKS 8.6B & 8.6C

Atoms Rearrange — and Density Helps Identify What's Left

A chemical change doesn't create or destroy atoms — it rearranges them into new combinations. When methane gas (CH₄) burns, it reacts with oxygen (O₂) in the air. The carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen atoms don't disappear; they regroup into carbon dioxide (CO₂) and water (H₂O).

C = Carbon H = Hydrogen O = Oxygen 1 carbon, 4 hydrogens, 4 oxygens — both before and after.

Density: a Fingerprint for Matter

Mass alone can't tell you what something is made of — a huge piece of foam can outweigh a small chunk of lead. But density (mass ÷ volume) stays the same no matter how big or small a sample is, which makes it a reliable clue for identifying a material.
